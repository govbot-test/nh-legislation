METHODOLOGY:
This bill legalizes the possession, use, and regulated sale of cannabis for individuals 21 years of
age and older and establishes a comprehensive state regulatory, enforcement, and revenue
framework. This bill assigns responsibility for the regulation and taxation of the cannabis
industry in New Hampshire to the Liquor Commission, which is renamed the Liquor and
Cannabis Commission. The Commission is responsible for adopting rules for the licensing and
regulation of cannabis cultivation facilities and other cannabis establishments, as well as for the
manufacture and sale of cannabis products and cannabis accessories. The Commission is
requiredtoestablishtheregulatoryandtaxframeworknotlaterthan12monthsafterpassageof
the act. The bill also creates a Cannabis Fund to support administration of the program, which
is expected to receive revenues from cannabis related taxes and fees, although the timing and
amount of such revenue are indeterminable. The bill provides General Fund appropriations to
the Liquor and Cannabis Commission, to be deposited into the Cannabis Fund, to support
startupandadministrativecostspriortothegenerationofcannabisrevenue.
AppropriationandFunding:
This bill makes the following appropriations: (To avoid double counting the appropriations, only the
generalfundappropriationsareincludedinthefiscalimpacttableabove.)
· $15,000,000fromtheCannabisFundtotheCannabisCommissionforthebiennium
endingJune30,2027foradministration.
· $7,000,000 for fiscal year ending June 30, 2026 and $8,000,000 for fiscal year ending
June 30, 2028 to the Liquor Commission for deposit into the Cannabis Fund for the
administrationofRSA318-F.
It appears the $15,000,000 appropriation from the Cannabis Fund to the Cannabis
Commission is intended to be supported by the General Fund appropriations provided
elsewhere in the bill. However, the General Fund appropriation of $8,000,000 does not
occur until FY 2028, which is after the biennium ending June 30, 2027 for which the
Cannabis Fund appropriation is authorized, creating a timing mismatch between the
funding sources. Additionally, if this bill is signed into law after June 30, 2026 the
$7,000,000 appropriation will not be enacted and no funds from the General Fund will be
depositedintotheCannabisFundfortheadministrationofRSA318-F.
· $500,000 for the biennium ending June 30, 2027 is appropriation from the Cannabis
FundtotheSubstanceUsePreventionandRecoveryFund.
· $500,000 for the biennium ending June 30, 2027 to the Substance Use Prevention and
Recovery Fund for the cost of developing and implementing a public eduction
campaign.
As written, the bill provides $1,000,000 to the Substance Use Prevention and Recovery
Fund for the biennium ending June 30, 2027, consisting of a $500,000 allocation from the
CannabisFundanda$500,000GeneralFundappropriation.
· $100,000 annually to the Department of Safety from the Cannabis Fund for FY 2027

Aswritten,thebillprovides$200,000totheDepartmentofSafetyinFY2027,consistingofa

Beginning in FY 2028 and each fiscal year thereafter, the bill provides $100,000 annually to
theDepartmentofSafetyfromtheCannabisFund.
The bill provides that, after deducting amounts charged to the Cannabis Fund for the cost of
administration, including appropriations to the Liquor and Cannabis Commission and specified
transfers to other agencies, the remaining revenue derived from the cannabis excise tax shall be
distributedasfollows:

Since the amount of cannabis sales, the timing of market implementation, and total revenue
deposited into the Cannabis Fund are not known, the amounts of future distributions cannot be
determined.
Revenues,FeesandFines
The bill imposes a 12.5% excise tax on wholesale transfers of cannabis to retail establishments.
The tax is administered through the regulatory framework established by the Liquor and
Cannabis Commission, with revenues deposited into the Cannabis Fund prior to distribution as
providedinthebill. Sincethetimingofmarketimplementation,thevolumeoftaxabletransfers,
and the price of cannabis products are not known, the amount of tax revenue that may be
generated under the bill is indeterminable. However, the Department of Revenue did a rough
estimate based on neighboring states, mainly Massachusetts, Maine and Vermont and estimates
the tax revenue could be between $27 million and $40 million annually. However, when the
impactofrevenuewillbefeltisindeterminable.
Additionally, this bill establishes various license fees, renewal fees, application fees, and other
regulatory fees associated with the cultivation, manufacture, testing, and retail sale of cannabis,
aswellasfinesandpenaltiesforviolationsofRSA318-F. Thebillprovidesthatfeescollectedby
the Liquor and Cannabis Commission are to be deposited into the Cannabis Fund and used for
the administration and enforcement of the chapter. The bill further provides that fines and
penalties imposed for violations of RSA 318-F are to be deposited into the General Fund, unless
otherwise specified. Since the number of licensees, the level of enforcement activity, and the
amount of fines and fees that may be collected are not known, the amount of revenue generated
fromfeesandfinescannotbedetermined.
Administration
The bill assigns responsibility for the regulation and oversight of adult use cannabis to the
Liquor Commission, which is renamed the Liquor and Cannabis Commission. The Commission
is responsible for administering RSA 318-F, including adopting rules, issuing and renewing
licenses, enforcing compliance, regulating cultivation, manufacturing, testing, and retail
cannabis establishments, and overseeing product safety, labeling, and inventory tracking. The
Commission is also responsible for administering the Cannabis Fund, collecting and depositing
fees and revenues as provided in the bill, and coordinating with other state agencies, including
theDepartmentofHealthandHumanServices,theDepartmentofRevenueAdministration,and
the Department of Safety. Administrative and enforcement activities are initially supported
through General Fund appropriations deposited into the Cannabis Fund, with ongoing
operations intended to be supported by Cannabis Fund revenues once the program is
operational.
The Department of Health and Human Services states the bill creates the Substance Abuse
Prevention and Recovery Fund to be administered by the Department in coordination with the
Governor’s Commission on Alcohol and Other Drugs. The Department is responsible for
administering and overseeing distributions from the fund for approved purposes related to
substance use prevention, treatment, recovery, training, public education, data collection, and
reporting. The billprovides aone-timeappropriation forthebiennium endingJune 30, 2027for
the development and implementation of a preliminary public education campaign prior to retail
cannabis sales. The Department indicates administration of the fund would require rulemaking
to establish program operations, qualifications, application and reporting requirements,
timelinesforprocessingapplications,andongoingoversightandevaluationoffundedprograms.
To carry out these responsibilities, the Department requests one Program Specialist IV (21-1090
MISC SOC SVC SPCS-6 SOC21-06, step 4) position to coordinate and manage the Substance
Abuse Prevention and Recovery Fund, including planning and development of programs, policy
and procedure development, grant administration, interagency coordination, data analysis, and
required reporting. The Department estimates the cost of this position at $104,000 in FY 2027,
$109,000 in FY 2028, and $114,000 in FY 2029. The Department further states there is
considerable uncertainty regarding the public health impacts of cannabis legalization, including
potential increases in substance use, treatment utilization, and prevention needs. While
indeterminable, the Department assumes future expenditures from the fund could be significant
anddependentonthelevelofcannabisrevenuegeneratedandprogramdemand.
The Department of Safety states the bill legalizes adult use cannabis and may result in
increasedenforcementrelatedactivities,includingimpaireddriving,drugmonitoring,andpublic
safety responses. The bill provides a General Fund appropriation of $100,000 for the fiscal year
endingJune30,2027nd$100,000annualappropriationfromtheCannabisFundforinformation
and analysis center activities, drug monitoring initiatives, and the collection of baseline data to
support required reporting. The Department indicates the overall enforcement related fiscal
impact of the bill is indeterminable, as it is not possible to predict changes in criminal activity,
impaireddrivingincidents,ortrainingandstaffingneedsassociatedwithlegalization.
The Division of Motor Vehicles, within the Department of Safety, states the bill establishes
penalties for consuming cannabis while operating or attempting to operate a motor vehicle,
includingfinesandpotentiallicensesuspensions. TheDivisionindicatesitsroleislimitedtothe
administrative processing of court ordered license suspensions. The Division estimates a one
time cost of approximately $70,000 in the fiscal year ending June 30, 2027 from the Highway
Fund for information technology system modifications necessary to implement the new
suspension provisions. The Division does not anticipate significant ongoing costs beyond initial
systemupdates.
The Department of Revenue Administration states the bill establishes a Cannabis Fund from
which distributions are made, including transfers to the Education Trust Fund. The
Department indicates its role is limited to fiscal accounting and ensuring such transfers are
reflected in the calculation of the Statewide Education Property Tax revenue target. The
Department states the timing and amount of cannabis revenue are indeterminable and that any
new responsibilities associated with these transfers can be absorbed within its current operating
budget.
The Judicial Branch states the bill makes substantial revisions to criminal laws related to
cannabis possession and establishes a regulatory framework for cannabis related offenses. The
Branch anticipates significant administrative work would be required to update case processing
systems, forms, instructions, and training materials. Initial implementation costs are
indeterminable but are estimated to be more than $10,000 and less than $100,000. Ongoing
costs after implementation are expected to be indeterminable but likely less than $10,000
annually. The Branch notes no funding is provided for these costs and indicates it may be
requiredtoreallocateexistingresources.
This bill adds, deletes, or modifies a criminal penalty, or changes statute to which there is a
penalty for violation. Therefore, this bill may have an impact on the judicial and correctional
systems, which could affect prosecution, incarceration, probation, and parole costs, for the state,
as well as county and local governments. A summary of such costs can be found at:
https://gencourt.state.nh.us/lba/Budget/Fiscal_Notes/JudicialCorrectionalCosts.pdf
The New Hampshire Municipal Association states the bill establishes procedures for the
legalization, regulation, and taxation of cannabis and the licensing and regulation of cannabis
establishments. The Association indicates municipalities may enact ordinances prohibiting or
limiting the number and type of cannabis establishments permitted within the municipality and
regulating the time, place, and manner of operation of such establishments. The bill further
provides that, after deducting the annual cost of administration, 5% of remaining cannabis
revenue is to be disbursed to public safety agencies, including police, fire, and rescue agencies,
for purposes including hiring and training additional drug recognition experts, advanced
roadsideimpaireddrivingenforcementtraining, andrespondingtodrugoverdoseincidents. The
Association states that municipalities are likely to experience indeterminable increases in costs
associated with legalization, including shifts in law enforcement focus and public safety
responses. While the bill provides a municipal revenue mechanism, the Association indicates it
is unclear whether revenues received by municipalities will offset the overall costs associated
with implementation, as both costs and revenues are dependent on enforcement activity,
businessdecisions,andmarketconditionsthatcannotbedeterminedatthistime.

METHODOLOGY:
This bill legalizes the possession, use, and regulated sale of cannabis for individuals 21 years of
age and older and establishes a comprehensive state regulatory, enforcement, and revenue
framework. This bill assigns responsibility for the regulation and taxation of the cannabis
industry in New Hampshire to the Liquor Commission, which is renamed the Liquor and
Cannabis Commission. The Commission is responsible for adopting rules for the licensing and

regulation of cannabis cultivation facilities and other cannabis establishments, as well as for the
manufacture and sale of cannabis products and cannabis accessories. The Commission is
requiredtoestablishtheregulatoryandtaxframeworknotlaterthan12monthsafterpassageof
the act. The bill also creates a Cannabis Fund to support administration of the program, which
is expected to receive revenues from cannabis related taxes and fees, although the timing and
amount of such revenue are indeterminable. The bill provides General Fund appropriations to
the Liquor and Cannabis Commission, to be deposited into the Cannabis Fund, to support
startupandadministrativecostspriortothegenerationofcannabisrevenue.
AppropriationandFunding:
This bill makes the following appropriations: (To avoid double counting the appropriations, only the
generalfundappropriationsareincludedinthefiscalimpacttableabove.)

endingJune30,2027foradministration.
· $7,000,000 for fiscal year ending June 30, 2026 and $8,000,000 for fiscal year ending
June 30, 2028 to the Liquor Commission for deposit into the Cannabis Fund for the
administrationofRSA318-F.
It appears the $15,000,000 appropriation from the Cannabis Fund to the Cannabis
Commission is intended to be supported by the General Fund appropriations provided
elsewhere in the bill. However, the General Fund appropriation of $8,000,000 does not
occur until FY 2028, which is after the biennium ending June 30, 2027 for which the
Cannabis Fund appropriation is authorized, creating a timing mismatch between the
funding sources. Additionally, if this bill is signed into law after June 30, 2026 the
$7,000,000 appropriation will not be enacted and no funds from the General Fund will be
depositedintotheCannabisFundfortheadministrationofRSA318-F.

The bill provides that, after deducting amounts charged to the Cannabis Fund for the cost of

Revenues,FeesandFines
The bill imposes a 12.5% excise tax on wholesale transfers of cannabis to retail establishments.
The tax is administered through the regulatory framework established by the Liquor and
Cannabis Commission, with revenues deposited into the Cannabis Fund prior to distribution as
providedinthebill. Sincethetimingofmarketimplementation,thevolumeoftaxabletransfers,
and the price of cannabis products are not known, the amount of tax revenue that may be
generated under the bill is indeterminable. However, the Department of Revenue did a rough
estimate based on neighboring states, mainly Massachusetts, Maine and Vermont and estimates
the tax revenue could be between $27 million and $40 million annually. However, when the
impactofrevenuewillbefeltisindeterminable.
Additionally, this bill establishes various license fees, renewal fees, application fees, and other
regulatory fees associated with the cultivation, manufacture, testing, and retail sale of cannabis,
aswellasfinesandpenaltiesforviolationsofRSA318-F. Thebillprovidesthatfeescollectedby
the Liquor and Cannabis Commission are to be deposited into the Cannabis Fund and used for

the administration and enforcement of the chapter. The bill further provides that fines and
penalties imposed for violations of RSA 318-F are to be deposited into the General Fund, unless
otherwise specified. Since the number of licensees, the level of enforcement activity, and the
amount of fines and fees that may be collected are not known, the amount of revenue generated
fromfeesandfinescannotbedetermined.
Administration
The bill assigns responsibility for the regulation and oversight of adult use cannabis to the
Liquor Commission, which is renamed the Liquor and Cannabis Commission. The Commission
is responsible for administering RSA 318-F, including adopting rules, issuing and renewing
licenses, enforcing compliance, regulating cultivation, manufacturing, testing, and retail
cannabis establishments, and overseeing product safety, labeling, and inventory tracking. The
Commission is also responsible for administering the Cannabis Fund, collecting and depositing
fees and revenues as provided in the bill, and coordinating with other state agencies, including
theDepartmentofHealthandHumanServices,theDepartmentofRevenueAdministration,and
the Department of Safety. Administrative and enforcement activities are initially supported
through General Fund appropriations deposited into the Cannabis Fund, with ongoing
operations intended to be supported by Cannabis Fund revenues once the program is
operational.
The Department of Health and Human Services states the bill creates the Substance Abuse
Prevention and Recovery Fund to be administered by the Department in coordination with the
Governor’s Commission on Alcohol and Other Drugs. The Department is responsible for
administering and overseeing distributions from the fund for approved purposes related to
substance use prevention, treatment, recovery, training, public education, data collection, and
reporting. The billprovides aone-timeappropriation forthebiennium endingJune 30, 2027for
the development and implementation of a preliminary public education campaign prior to retail
cannabis sales. The Department indicates administration of the fund would require rulemaking
to establish program operations, qualifications, application and reporting requirements,
timelinesforprocessingapplications,andongoingoversightandevaluationoffundedprograms.
To carry out these responsibilities, the Department requests one Program Specialist IV (21-1090
MISC SOC SVC SPCS-6 SOC21-06, step 4) position to coordinate and manage the Substance
Abuse Prevention and Recovery Fund, including planning and development of programs, policy
and procedure development, grant administration, interagency coordination, data analysis, and
required reporting. The Department estimates the cost of this position at $104,000 in FY 2027,
$109,000 in FY 2028, and $114,000 in FY 2029. The Department further states there is
considerable uncertainty regarding the public health impacts of cannabis legalization, including
potential increases in substance use, treatment utilization, and prevention needs. While

indeterminable, the Department assumes future expenditures from the fund could be significant
anddependentonthelevelofcannabisrevenuegeneratedandprogramdemand.
The Department of Safety states the bill legalizes adult use cannabis and may result in
increasedenforcementrelatedactivities,includingimpaireddriving,drugmonitoring,andpublic
safety responses. The bill provides a General Fund appropriation of $100,000 for the fiscal year
endingJune30,2027nd$100,000annualappropriationfromtheCannabisFundforinformation
and analysis center activities, drug monitoring initiatives, and the collection of baseline data to
support required reporting. The Department indicates the overall enforcement related fiscal
impact of the bill is indeterminable, as it is not possible to predict changes in criminal activity,
impaireddrivingincidents,ortrainingandstaffingneedsassociatedwithlegalization.
The Division of Motor Vehicles, within the Department of Safety, states the bill establishes
penalties for consuming cannabis while operating or attempting to operate a motor vehicle,
includingfinesandpotentiallicensesuspensions. TheDivisionindicatesitsroleislimitedtothe
administrative processing of court ordered license suspensions. The Division estimates a one
time cost of approximately $70,000 in the fiscal year ending June 30, 2027 from the Highway
Fund for information technology system modifications necessary to implement the new
suspension provisions. The Division does not anticipate significant ongoing costs beyond initial
systemupdates.
The Department of Revenue Administration states the bill establishes a Cannabis Fund from
which distributions are made, including transfers to the Education Trust Fund. The
Department indicates its role is limited to fiscal accounting and ensuring such transfers are
reflected in the calculation of the Statewide Education Property Tax revenue target. The
Department states the timing and amount of cannabis revenue are indeterminable and that any
new responsibilities associated with these transfers can be absorbed within its current operating
budget.
The Judicial Branch states the bill makes substantial revisions to criminal laws related to
cannabis possession and establishes a regulatory framework for cannabis related offenses. The
Branch anticipates significant administrative work would be required to update case processing
systems, forms, instructions, and training materials. Initial implementation costs are
indeterminable but are estimated to be more than $10,000 and less than $100,000. Ongoing
costs after implementation are expected to be indeterminable but likely less than $10,000
annually. The Branch notes no funding is provided for these costs and indicates it may be
requiredtoreallocateexistingresources.
This bill adds, deletes, or modifies a criminal penalty, or changes statute to which there is a
penalty for violation. Therefore, this bill may have an impact on the judicial and correctional

systems, which could affect prosecution, incarceration, probation, and parole costs, for the state,
as well as county and local governments. A summary of such costs can be found at:
https://gencourt.state.nh.us/lba/Budget/Fiscal_Notes/JudicialCorrectionalCosts.pdf
The New Hampshire Municipal Association states the bill establishes procedures for the
legalization, regulation, and taxation of cannabis and the licensing and regulation of cannabis
establishments. The Association indicates municipalities may enact ordinances prohibiting or
limiting the number and type of cannabis establishments permitted within the municipality and
regulating the time, place, and manner of operation of such establishments. The bill further
provides that, after deducting the annual cost of administration, 5% of remaining cannabis
revenue is to be disbursed to public safety agencies, including police, fire, and rescue agencies,
for purposes including hiring and training additional drug recognition experts, advanced
roadsideimpaireddrivingenforcementtraining, andrespondingtodrugoverdoseincidents. The
Association states that municipalities are likely to experience indeterminable increases in costs
associated with legalization, including shifts in law enforcement focus and public safety
responses. While the bill provides a municipal revenue mechanism, the Association indicates it
is unclear whether revenues received by municipalities will offset the overall costs associated
with implementation, as both costs and revenues are dependent on enforcement activity,
businessdecisions,andmarketconditionsthatcannotbedeterminedatthistime.
